Why This Plan Exists

A degree built from three
other degrees

Robotics & AI stitches together Mechanical Engineering, Electrical Engineering, and Computer Science into one four-year program.

That's a real structural risk. Spend four years sampling a little of everything, and it's possible to graduate knowing the names of many things — and being genuinely good at none of them.

Recruiters and interviewers notice instantly. So does the work, the moment it gets hard.

The Model

Picture the letter T

The horizontal bar is breadth — a working, conversational grasp of many areas. Enough to talk to anyone on a team and see how the pieces connect.

The vertical bar is depth — one area where she is genuinely, defensibly good. Good enough that teammates come to her.

The curriculum hands her the horizontal bar automatically, every semester. It does not hand her the vertical bar. She has to build that herself.

The Honest Check

The 45-minute test

"Could I survive 45 minutes of someone pushing hard on just this one topic — asking me to derive it, debug it, or extend it to a new situation — without running out of real things to say?"

YES → DEPTH
She can work inside the topic — extend it, debug it, push back on it.
NO → STILL BREADTH
She can describe what it is, but not operate confidently within it — however interesting it feels.

Run this test every year to check whether she's specializing — or just collecting topics.

Year-by-Year Plan
01
YEAR OF FOUR
Explore honestly
  • Take core courses seriously — math, programming, intro mechanics, electronics. The foundation every vertical sits on.
  • Try a small project in 2–3 different areas. Goal isn't mastery — it's data about herself.
  • Notice where time disappears in a good way — where she keeps going past what was assigned.
  • Fine to not know her lane yet. Not fine to still not know by year 3.
By year-end, answer:
"What's the one thing I'm better at than most people in my batch?" Even a tentative answer is enough to act on.

Year-by-Year Plan
02
YEAR OF FOUR
Narrow down
  • Choose electives from one vertical, not one of each — stack toward perception, or controls, not a sampler.
  • Go deeper into the math specific to that vertical (optimization for ML, control theory for dynamics).
  • Start a personal project entirely her own, inside the chosen vertical — not a tutorial clone.
  • Join a club, competition, or research group working in that area. Depth grows faster around others doing the same hard thing.
By year-end, she should say:
"I'm the person in my friend group who's actually good at ___." Not "I do robotics and AI."

Year-by-Year Plan
03
YEAR OF FOUR
Go deep, on purpose
  • Seek an internship or research role under one mentor, on one hard problem, for months. The single highest-leverage move this year.
  • Resist "well-rounded" internships that rotate across three teams — that's a year-1 move, not year-3.
  • Keep just enough breadth to integrate with the rest of the system — don't let it compete with depth for her best hours.
  • Start reading papers in her vertical, even when some of it goes over her head at first.
By year-end, she should:
Explain her sub-area to a skeptical senior engineer — and hold her ground when they push back.

Year-by-Year Plan
04
YEAR OF FOUR
Prove it
  • Capstone must sit unambiguously inside her vertical — and go deep on the hardest sub-problem in it.
  • If the project naturally touches other areas, say so honestly: "I built X myself; I used existing tools for Y and Z." That's a strength, not a weakness.
  • Avoid the project that tries to prove "I can do mechanical AND electrical AND AI" — that's the jack-of-three outcome this whole plan avoids.
  • Be ready to discuss failures and what she'd do differently — that's what separates a real project from a checkbox.
Decide what's next using her vertical — not her degree title — as the deciding factor.

What Comes After

Job, M.Tech, or both

No single right answer — it depends on how clear the vertical feels by year 4, and the kind of work she wants to be doing by 2030–2032.

OPTION A
Straight to industry
Best if
Her vertical is something companies are hiring for right now, and year-3/4 projects already show real fluency.
  • Strongest path to build real products quickly while learning on the job.
  • Indian robotics firms increasingly hire for narrow, demonstrable skills over generic "robotics" knowledge.
OPTION B
M.Tech in her vertical
Best if
Her vertical leans research-heavy (planning, RL, advanced perception) or she wants harder, less-templated problems.
  • Pick the specialization matching her vertical — not a generic "Robotics" M.Tech that repeats the breadth problem.
  • Avoid using an M.Tech to "decide later." It should deepen one lane, not extend sampling.
OPTION C
M.Tech + industry
Best if
She wants postgraduate depth and credibility, but also real deployment experience first.
  • Common low-risk path: 1–2 years industry first to confirm the fit, then M.Tech once the direction is clear.

Before You Go

A few honest reminders

01
It's okay to be undecided in year 1. It's a flag worth a real conversation if still undecided by year 3.
02
Math and fundamentals aren't "breadth" — they're the base the T stands on. Tools change by 2030; linear algebra and control theory don't.
03
One deep project beats five shallow ones — every time, in every interview, in every application.
04
The degree gives breadth by default. Nobody gives depth. She builds it, on purpose, starting now.
