## Slide 2: What is Information Science Engineering?

### Core Definition
Information Science Engineering is the study of how information is created, organised, stored, secured, retrieved, and used by people, organisations, and systems. It combines computer science fundamentals with a focused emphasis on data management, information systems design, and applied software engineering.

### The IS vs CSE Distinction

**CSE (Computer Science Engineering):** Builds computing systems — how computers work, how compilers are designed, how operating systems manage resources, how algorithms are made efficient.

**IS (Information Science Engineering):** Organises and extracts value from information — how databases are designed, how large datasets are managed, how business systems (ERP, CRM) work, how information flows securely across networks.

Both are strong branches. IS is more applied and industry-facing; CSE is deeper in theory and computer science fundamentals. In VTU's 2025 scheme, the gap has narrowed significantly — IS now includes Machine Learning, Big Data Analytics, Full Stack Development, and Information Security as core subjects.

### Three Pillars of IS

**Data:** Databases, big data systems, data science, data governance, information retrieval.

**Systems:** Software systems, ERP platforms, web and enterprise applications, cloud systems.

**People:** Systems analysis, requirement gathering, stakeholder communication, user-centred design.

### Key Insight
IS graduates appear for **GATE CSE** — the same paper as Computer Science Engineering graduates. This means the same M.Tech and NIC Scientist-B eligibility, the same top-tier higher education paths, and the same placement pools at major IT companies.

### Speaker Notes

Let us start by understanding what Information Science Engineering actually is — and more importantly, how it differs from CSE, because this distinction shapes your entire career path.

Think about what happens when you walk into a hospital. The doctor opens your patient file on a computer. It shows your medical history, past prescriptions, allergies, test results — all organised, all immediately accessible, all linked to your identity. Someone had to design that database. Someone had to figure out how to structure the relationships between patients, doctors, appointments, diagnoses, and billing. Someone had to secure it so that only authorised staff can access sensitive records. Someone had to make sure the system handles thousands of simultaneous queries without crashing. That person is an Information Science engineer.

Or think about a bank. When you transfer money via UPI, your transaction goes through multiple systems — your bank's core banking system, the NPCI gateway, the recipient bank's system. Each of these systems stores, validates, and transmits information. The databases, the network protocols, the security layers, the transaction logs — all of this is IS territory.

The three-pillar framework — Data, Systems, People — is what distinguishes IS from pure CSE. CSE engineers often focus deeply on the technical system. IS engineers are trained to also think about the information the system carries and the people who need to use it. This makes IS engineers particularly effective in roles that require translating between business requirements and technical implementations — roles like Systems Analyst, Business Analyst, and IT Consultant.

The IS vs CSE question is one of the most common decisions students face. Here is an honest comparison. CSE students go deeper into Theory of Computation, compiler design, OS internals, and advanced algorithms. IS students go deeper into database systems, big data analytics, information security, and enterprise application development. In practice, both appear for the same placements, the same GATE paper, and the same M.Tech programs. What matters more is your aptitude and interest. If you love database design, system architecture, and the applied side of technology, IS is an excellent choice. If you want to do deep algorithmic research or low-level systems programming, CSE is the stronger track.

One important point for students at VTU and similar universities: the 2025 IS scheme is notably stronger than the older 2021 scheme. Machine Learning, which was previously an elective, is now a core subject in Semester 5. Big Data Analytics, previously Semester 7 elective, is now core in Semester 6. The curriculum is genuinely modern and industry-relevant.

- **Data Structures and Applications** *(PCC)* — Arrays, linked lists, stacks, queues, trees, graphs, sorting, searching

### Speaker Notes

Let us walk through what you actually study in the first two years of Information Science Engineering at VTU, which is the most common IS program in Karnataka and one of the largest IS programs in India.

First year is common to all VTU engineering branches. While it might feel generic, each subject has direct relevance to IS. Engineering Mathematics — particularly probability and statistics — becomes essential when you study Machine Learning, Big Data Analytics, and database query optimisation later. The reason JOIN operations and database indexes work efficiently is rooted in mathematics. Linear algebra underlies much of machine learning. Probability theory is foundational to data science.

Programming in C in first year is your first experience of computational thinking — the ability to break a problem into steps a computer can execute. This is arguably the most transferable skill you will develop in four years. Even if you never write C professionally, the discipline of structured problem-solving carries through to Python, Java, SQL, and everything else you will learn.

Second year is where IS specialisation genuinely begins, and the subjects chosen are strategically important.

Object-Oriented Programming with Java is not merely a programming course — it introduces a fundamental paradigm that underlies most enterprise software. Java is the language of Android, of Spring Boot enterprise applications, of banking systems, and of large-scale backend platforms. Learning OOP properly — understanding encapsulation, inheritance, polymorphism — prepares you for database-backed applications and enterprise systems you will encounter in your career.

Operating Systems is more important for IS than students initially realise. Database management systems sit on top of operating systems — understanding process scheduling helps you understand query execution, understanding memory management helps you understand buffer pools in databases, understanding file systems helps you understand how data is stored on disk. OS fundamentals are tested in GATE CSE and in interviews at top IT companies.

Data Structures and Applications is one of the most important courses of your entire program. Every database index is a tree. Every shortest-path query on a map uses graph algorithms. Every search query uses some form of hashing. Understanding data structures deeply — not just implementing them, but knowing why each exists and when to use which — is what separates good IS engineers from weak ones.

The new 2025 addition of Project Management with Git in Semester 3 is particularly practical. Git is the universal version control tool used by every software company on earth. Learning it in Semester 3 means all your subsequent projects — labs, mini-projects, capstone — can use professional version control practices. Agile and Scrum are the dominant project management methodologies in IT services, product companies, and startups. Starting this exposure in Semester 3 is a genuine improvement over the older curriculum.

### Speaker Notes

Third year is where IS reveals its character — a curriculum that is simultaneously applied and rigorous, covering the technologies that actually power organisations.

Semester 4 centres on Database Management Systems, and rightfully so. DBMS is the single most IS-defining subject in the entire curriculum. You learn how to model real-world entities and their relationships using ER (Entity-Relationship) diagrams — the process of thinking about a business domain and translating it into a structured data model. You learn SQL comprehensively: not just SELECT queries, but complex joins, subqueries, stored procedures, triggers, and transactions. You learn normalisation — the process of structuring tables to eliminate redundancy and maintain data integrity. You learn how indexes speed up queries and how the query optimiser decides how to execute a SQL statement efficiently.

This subject directly maps to professional roles. Database Administrator, Data Analyst, Systems Analyst, Data Engineer — all of these require deep SQL and DBMS knowledge. Every IT company — TCS, Infosys, Oracle, SAP, banks — uses relational databases every day. In your GATE CSE exam, DBMS is one of the highest-weightage topics. In technical interviews, SQL problems are universal. DBMS is an investment that pays dividends for your entire career.

Computer Networks is equally important because information does not sit still — it travels across networks. Understanding TCP/IP, routing, DNS, and network security gives you the foundation for cloud computing, information security, and distributed systems later. Every web application you build requires network knowledge to deploy properly, to secure, and to diagnose when it fails.

Design and Analysis of Algorithms is the course that builds your problem-solving depth. It is the primary subject tested in GATE CSE and in technical interviews at Google, Amazon, Microsoft, and Flipkart. Students who invest in this subject — genuinely understanding dynamic programming, graph algorithms, and complexity analysis — open the doors to product company placements with ₹15-40 LPA packages.

Semester 5 is the strongest semester in the IS curriculum, with three practically powerful subjects.

Machine Learning in Semester 5 as a core IPCC is the most significant change in the VTU 2025 IS scheme. You implement linear regression, logistic regression, decision trees, random forests, K-means clustering, and neural networks. You use Python with libraries like NumPy, pandas, and scikit-learn. Understanding ML is becoming baseline literacy for IS engineers — not to do research ML, but to use ML tools for data classification, fraud detection, recommendation engines, and anomaly detection in systems you build or manage.

Full Stack Development is an IS speciality that makes graduates immediately productive in their first job. You build complete web applications from database to frontend — designing the React UI, writing Express/Node.js backend logic, connecting to a MongoDB or PostgreSQL database, securing with JWT authentication, and deploying to AWS or Heroku. This is exactly what companies need from fresh IS graduates.

The Hackathon-Based Project in 2025 scheme is a significant addition. Building a working prototype under time pressure, presenting it to judges, and handling real feedback is closer to industry experience than any exam. Students who treat hackathons seriously often build the best portfolio projects.

### Speaker Notes

Fourth year in IS is where the curriculum becomes genuinely advanced and IS-specific, with subjects that directly define the professional identity of IS engineers.

Semester 6 is the most distinctively IS semester in the entire program. Three of its core subjects — Big Data Analytics, Information and Network Security, and Data Science and Visualization — are IS-specific additions that do not appear in the standard CSE curriculum. Together, they give IS graduates a data and security competency that is increasingly valuable in 2026.

Big Data Analytics addresses the reality that modern organisations generate data at scales that traditional relational databases cannot handle alone. You learn the Hadoop ecosystem: HDFS (the distributed file system), MapReduce (the computation model), Apache Hive (SQL-like queries over Hadoop), and Pig. More importantly, you learn Apache Spark — the modern, fast, in-memory processing framework that has largely replaced MapReduce for most production workloads. You also cover NoSQL databases: MongoDB for document-oriented data, Cassandra for time-series and high-availability scenarios. Companies like Flipkart, Amazon, Zomato, and HDFC Bank run big data systems that process terabytes daily — IS graduates with Spark and NoSQL skills are directly employable in data engineering roles.

Information and Network Security is IS's most unique speciality. You study cryptography from fundamentals — symmetric encryption (AES, DES), asymmetric encryption (RSA, ECC), hash functions (SHA-256, MD5), and how they combine in TLS/SSL to secure HTTPS. You understand PKI and digital certificates. You learn about firewalls, IDS, VPN, and network attack types. This subject directly maps to careers in cybersecurity — one of the highest-demand, highest-paying specialisations in IT today. India's DPDP Act (2023) has created a wave of compliance and data security roles. Students who take this subject seriously and add CompTIA Security+, CEH, or CISSP certifications can command significant salary premiums.

Data Science and Visualization teaches you to extract insights from data and communicate them visually. This is the practical skill that makes Data Analysts valuable — not just running SQL queries, but building dashboards in Tableau or Power BI, telling a story with data, and helping stakeholders make decisions. In industry, data insights that nobody can read or act on are worthless. The ability to visualise and communicate data is what separates useful analysts from ones who just produce reports nobody reads.

Cloud Computing is foundational for all IS professionals in 2026. Virtually every production system — whether a startup, a bank, or a government department — runs on AWS, Azure, or GCP. You learn how to deploy applications to the cloud, configure managed databases, set up load balancers, manage access control, and use containerisation with Docker. An IS graduate who has hands-on AWS experience — and ideally an AWS Cloud Practitioner or Solutions Architect certification — is dramatically more employable than one without.

The Generative AI elective in Semester 6 is a forward-looking addition. Prompt engineering, RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) pipelines, and building business applications on top of LLMs (like GPT-4 or Claude APIs) are emerging skills. IS engineers integrating LLMs into information systems — building document retrieval chatbots, intelligent ERP assistants, automated compliance checkers — represent a growing professional opportunity.

High Performance Computing in Semester 7 is IS-specific and technically deep. Understanding parallel processing, GPU computing, and distributed computation prepares graduates for roles in data engineering and ML infrastructure — areas that deal with large-scale data processing. It also deepens understanding of cloud-scale systems.

The 15-week mandatory internship in Semester 8 is the most industry-oriented feature of the VTU IS program. Real company exposure — writing production code, using professional tools, working in agile teams, attending client calls — bridges the gap between academic knowledge and professional practice more effectively than any additional coursework. Students who treat the internship seriously often convert it into a Pre-Placement Offer (PPO).

## Slide 6: Skills You'll Develop

### Technical Skills

**Database Design and Management**
Design relational schemas, write complex SQL queries, optimise database performance, manage transactions and concurrency, work with NoSQL databases (MongoDB, Cassandra), and handle database administration tasks. This is the deepest IS-specific technical competency.

**Full Stack Application Development**
Build complete web applications — React or Angular frontends, Node.js or Django/Flask backends, REST API design, PostgreSQL or MongoDB databases, and cloud deployment. Cover the entire stack from UI to database to server.

**Data Engineering and Analytics**
Build data pipelines using Apache Spark and Kafka, perform ETL (Extract-Transform-Load) processes, create dashboards with Tableau or Power BI, and apply statistical and ML methods for business insights.

**Information Security**
Understand cryptographic protocols, configure firewalls and VPNs, perform basic vulnerability assessments, implement secure coding practices, and understand compliance requirements (ISO 27001, India's DPDP Act).

**Cloud Computing and DevOps**
Deploy applications to AWS/Azure/GCP, configure cloud storage and compute, use Docker containers, set up CI/CD pipelines with GitHub Actions or Jenkins, and monitor production systems.

**Systems Analysis and Requirements Engineering**
Translate business requirements into technical specifications, model systems using UML (use case, sequence, ER diagrams), conduct stakeholder interviews, and manage project documentation in tools like Jira and Confluence.

### Tools You Will Use Daily

**Development:** VS Code, IntelliJ IDEA, Git / GitHub, Postman, Docker

**Data:** MySQL, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Apache Spark, Python (pandas, scikit-learn), Tableau, Power BI

**Cloud:** AWS (EC2, S3, RDS, Lambda), Azure, Google Cloud

**Security:** Wireshark, Nmap, Burp Suite (for learning ethical hacking)

**Project Management:** Jira, Confluence, draw.io, MS Teams

### Speaker Notes

Skills in IS engineering are best understood as layered — a technical core, a data and analytics layer, a communication and analysis layer, and a systems thinking layer. Together, they make IS engineers versatile professionals who can function in multiple roles across multiple industries.

Database design and management is the IS engineer's signature competency. While CSE students learn databases as one subject among many, IS students go deeper and apply database knowledge across more subjects. In DBMS, you design schemas and write SQL. In Big Data Analytics, you deal with data at petabyte scale using Hadoop and Spark. In Data Science, you query and analyse databases for insights. In Information Security, you protect databases from SQL injection and unauthorised access. In the Capstone Project, you design the entire data architecture of your system. This depth means IS graduates are better positioned than most for Database Administrator, Data Engineer, and Systems Analyst roles.

Full stack development skills make IS graduates immediately productive. The VTU 2025 curriculum — Full Stack Development as a core subject in Semester 5 — is well-aligned with industry demand. Companies recruit IS freshers expecting them to contribute to web application development from day one. Understanding both the frontend (React, HTML/CSS, JavaScript) and the backend (Node.js or Django, REST APIs, database integration) lets you work on complete features independently, which is valuable in both small startups and large IT services firms.

Data engineering and analytics is one of the fastest-growing IS specialisations. The ability to build pipelines that take raw data from multiple sources, clean and transform it, load it into a warehouse or lake, and produce reports and dashboards is in high demand. NASSCOM reports that India's analytics market is growing at over 25% annually. IS graduates who invest in learning Spark, Kafka, and cloud data warehouse tools (BigQuery, Redshift, Snowflake) can enter data engineering roles paying significantly above the average IS starting salary.

Information security skills set IS apart from most other engineering branches. With cybercrime and data breaches increasing, every organisation needs people who understand how to protect information. The IS curriculum's emphasis on cryptography, network security, and ethical hacking gives graduates a foundation that can be extended with professional certifications like CEH or CISSP into a dedicated security career track. India's DPDP Act 2023 — the first comprehensive data protection law — is creating specific demand for privacy engineering and data compliance roles.

Systems analysis and requirements engineering is the soft-technical skill that makes IS engineers uniquely valuable in client-facing roles. Being able to sit with a bank's operations team, understand their loan processing workflow, translate that into UML diagrams, and communicate it clearly to a development team — this is a rare and valued skill. UML modelling, requirement documentation in Jira, and stakeholder management are practiced throughout the IS curriculum and directly applicable in Systems Analyst and Business Analyst roles.

### Speaker Notes

Let me give you an honest, realistic picture of where Information Science graduates begin their careers and what the work actually involves.

The Systems Analyst is the most IS-native entry-level role. This is the person who sits between the business (the client, the operations team, the finance department) and the development team. When HDFC Bank wants to build a new loan processing module, a Systems Analyst interviews the bank's loan officers, documents every step of the process, identifies what information needs to be captured and how, draws ER diagrams for the database design, creates use case diagrams showing how users interact with the system, and writes the functional specification document that developers use to build. This role requires both technical literacy and strong communication skills — you need to understand what the developer can and cannot build, and you need to explain technical constraints to non-technical stakeholders. IS's emphasis on UML, requirements engineering, and database design directly prepares graduates for this role.

Database Administrator is one of the most stable and consistently in-demand IS career paths. Every organisation that stores data — which is every organisation — needs people who can design, manage, and optimise databases. Junior DBAs typically handle routine maintenance: running backups, monitoring query performance, granting user access, applying patches. As you gain experience, you take on performance tuning (analysing slow queries and optimising indexes), high availability design (setting up read replicas and failover), and eventually full database architecture. Oracle, SAP, and banking IT departments are particularly strong employers for DBA roles.

Software Developer and Full Stack Developer roles are the highest-volume hiring category. IS graduates compete here with CSE graduates, and the competition is real. The advantage for IS graduates who have invested in their Full Stack Development subject and built good portfolio projects is genuine — practical development skills matter more than branch label in this category. These roles involve writing features for web applications, working in Agile sprints, doing code reviews, and deploying changes to production. The quality of your GitHub portfolio and your performance in coding assessments determines which companies you get into.

The IT Consultant track — particularly SAP and ERP consulting — is IS-specific and highly lucrative over the long term. SAP consultants configure and customise SAP modules (Finance, HR, Supply Chain, Manufacturing) for enterprise clients. At the junior level, this involves mapping client processes to SAP standard functions, configuring tables and workflows, testing transactions, and documenting changes. SAP consultants who specialise in modules like SAP FICO (Finance & Controlling), SAP MM (Materials Management), or SAP SD (Sales & Distribution) become highly sought-after specialists. The market is large, stable, and not easily automated — it requires domain knowledge plus technical skills plus client communication ability.

The NIC (National Informatics Centre) Scientist-B track deserves special mention for IS graduates. NIC is the Government of India's premier IT organisation, responsible for GSTN (GST Network), NeSDA (e-governance), Aadhaar support systems, NIC email for all government officials, and digital India infrastructure. In 2026, 243 Scientist-B posts were advertised, filled purely through GATE CSE or GATE DA score. IS graduates are fully eligible. The salary is Level 10 of the 7th Pay Commission — ₹56,100 to ₹1,77,500 per month base, plus allowances, with excellent job security, government housing, and a very good work-life balance. For students who want a stable government career in the technology sector, NIC is one of the best options available.

## Slide 11: A Day in the Life — Systems Analyst at IT Services Firm (Bengaluru)

### The Workday

**8:30 AM — Daily Stand-Up (15 min)**
Sprint review in Jira with the development team. Each person shares what they completed yesterday, what they are working on today, and any blockers. You note that the backend team is delayed on the API because the DB schema changed — you need to update the specification document.

**9:00 AM — Stakeholder Emails & Communication**
Read emails from the client's operations team. The client wants to add a field to the loan application form capturing the borrower's employment type. You flag this as a scope change, draft a Change Request document, and email it to your project manager for approval before acknowledging the client.

**9:30 AM — Requirements Gathering Session**
Video call with the client's loan processing team — 4 people across two cities. You ask structured questions about how they currently handle exceptions (applications that don't fit standard categories), document the edge cases, and capture the data fields they need captured. You draw a preliminary ER diagram during the call in draw.io.

**11:00 AM — UML Documentation**
Update the use case diagram to include the new employment type flow. Update the sequence diagram showing how the updated form data flows to the underwriting system. These go into the BRD (Business Requirements Document) in Confluence.

**1:00 PM — DB Schema Review with DBA**
30-minute meeting with the database administrator to review the ER model. She flags that the employment type field should be a lookup table (not a free-text field) to maintain referential integrity and enable reporting. You agree — you update the ER diagram and note the change.

**2:00 PM — UAT (User Acceptance Testing) Session**
Walk the client's loan officers through 3 new features deployed to the test environment. They find one bug (incorrect validation on loan amount field — it accepts negative values). You log it in Jira, assign to developer, mark severity as Medium.

**3:30 PM — BRD Update and Sign-Off**
Update the BRD with today's changes. Send updated BRD to the client for review and sign-off. Without sign-off, the development team cannot build.

**4:30 PM — Offshore Dev Team Sync**
Quick call with the Mumbai-based development team to clarify a technical question about the API response format. They want to return the employment type as an integer code — you explain that the client's reporting tool expects a string label, so the API must return both the code and the label.

**5:30 PM — Status Report and Close**
Update the project tracker (% completion, RAG status — Red / Amber / Green). Send the daily status report to your project manager. Commit updated BRD and diagrams to Confluence.

**Tools Used Daily:** Jira, Confluence, draw.io, SQL (for data validation on the test DB), MS Excel, MS Teams, Outlook

### Speaker Notes

This day-in-the-life gives you a realistic picture of what a Systems Analyst actually does at an IT services firm working on a banking project — one of the most common IS employment scenarios.

The first thing to notice is that this is not primarily a coding role. You are writing SQL queries to validate data in the test environment, and you understand the technical architecture, but most of your output is structured documentation: BRDs, UML diagrams, change requests, test cases, and status reports. This is what IS trains you to produce. The ability to model a business process in UML, structure a database schema from a business requirement, and communicate technically to developers while also communicating clearly to business stakeholders is rare and valuable.

The Client-Stakeholder relationship is central. You are simultaneously managing the client's expectations — explaining what can be built by when, handling scope changes properly through Change Request processes rather than letting developers just add features informally — while also protecting the development team from ambiguous or changing requirements. This requires diplomatic skill: being honest about constraints without alienating the client, being firm about scope without being obstructive.

The Change Request process illustrated here — when the client asks for a new field, you do not just say yes — is standard professional practice. In IT services, uncontrolled scope changes destroy project margins. A Systems Analyst who understands this and handles scope changes properly is worth significantly more to the organisation than one who just collects requirements without controlling scope.

The interaction with the DBA about the employment type field illustrates how IS engineers collaborate. You bring the business knowledge (what the client needs), the DBA brings the database design expertise (why a lookup table is better than free text). This collaborative model — IS as the bridge between business requirements and technical implementation — is why IS engineers are hired for exactly this role.

The offshore dev team sync is typical of IT services delivery. Many Indian IT firms have development teams distributed across cities or countries. The Systems Analyst's role in clarifying requirements — in this case, the API response format — prevents expensive rework. A developer who misunderstands the response format and builds it incorrectly wastes days of work. Clear, precise technical specifications from the Systems Analyst prevent this.

This day is not glamorous. It involves a lot of documentation, meetings, emails, and careful communication. What makes it rewarding is the clear impact: the system you are helping to build will process hundreds of thousands of loan applications per year. When a farmer in a remote district gets a loan in 48 hours because the bank's system works correctly and efficiently, your specification document is part of the reason it happened.

## Slide 14: AI Impact — Disruption to IS Work

### What AI Is Automating

**SQL Query Generation**
GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT, and tools like Text-to-SQL (built into Tableau, BigQuery, AWS QuickSight) can generate SQL queries from natural language descriptions. A junior Data Analyst asking "show me the top 10 customers by revenue this quarter by region" used to require writing SQL; now AI can generate that query.

**Low-Code / No-Code Platforms**
Microsoft Power Apps, Salesforce Lightning, OutSystems, and similar platforms allow business users to build forms, workflows, and simple applications without writing code. This reduces the need for junior IS developers for routine application development.

**Automated Testing**
Tosca (included in VTU 2025 as an AEC Lab option), Selenium with AI extensions, and tools like Testim and Mabl can generate and run test cases automatically. The AI analyses the application UI and generates test scripts — work that junior QA engineers previously did manually.

**RPA (Robotic Process Automation)**
UiPath, Automation Anywhere, and Blue Prism automate repetitive data entry, form filling, report generation, and system-to-system data transfer. IS roles involving manual data processing are being reduced.

**BI Self-Service**
Power BI, Tableau, and Looker have AI-assisted analytics — natural language questions generate visualisations automatically. This reduces demand for manual report-building analysts.

### What This Means for Entry-Level IS Jobs

The entry-level roles most at risk are those involving **routine, rule-based information handling** — manual report building, simple SQL scripting, basic data entry, and boilerplate application development. Students targeting IS must aim for roles involving system design, data architecture, security, or complex analysis — not routine data handling.

### Speaker Notes

Honest assessment of AI's impact on IS is essential for students entering the workforce in 2026 and beyond.

SQL query generation by AI is real and worth understanding clearly. For simple queries — "SELECT revenue by region WHERE quarter = Q1" — AI generates correct SQL reliably. For complex queries involving multi-level JOINs, window functions, CTEs (Common Table Expressions), and performance-sensitive operations on 100-million-row tables, AI generates suggestions that require expert review and often correction. The skill that remains valuable is not writing basic SQL — it is knowing whether the AI's SQL is correct, optimal, and secure. This requires understanding how databases execute queries, what query plans look like, and when an index will help or hurt. Deep DBMS knowledge remains essential precisely because it is what lets you evaluate and improve AI-generated SQL.

Low-code/no-code platforms are reducing demand for junior developers building simple CRUD (Create-Read-Update-Delete) applications and internal tools. However, these platforms have significant limitations: they do not handle high-performance requirements well, they create vendor lock-in, they cannot implement complex custom business logic easily, and they often require integration with other systems through APIs — which requires traditional IS skills. The concern about low-code platforms is real for routine application development, but it does not extend to complex system design, data engineering, or security architecture.

Automated testing tools like Tosca (which VTU has wisely included in the 2025 curriculum) are genuinely replacing manual test case execution. But they are not replacing test strategy, test design, or QA leadership. Someone still needs to define what is tested, review the test cases the AI generates, analyse test failures, and make decisions about acceptable quality. QA engineers who understand testing strategy and move up to QA lead or test architect roles are not at risk; those who only do manual test execution are.

RPA is the AI impact area most directly relevant to IS. UiPath and Automation Anywhere are replacing data entry roles, form processing roles, and inter-system data transfer tasks that were previously done by junior IT operations staff. However, someone needs to design the RPA robots, configure them, handle the exceptions the robots cannot process, and maintain them when the underlying systems change. RPA developer and RPA architect are now legitimate IS careers. Understanding which roles are being automated and pivoting to the roles that manage and design automation is the correct strategic response.

The net assessment: IS as a field is not being automated away — it is being reshaped. The work is shifting from manual information handling toward system design, data architecture, security, and AI integration. Students who understand this shift and develop skills in the higher-value areas — cloud architecture, data engineering, information security, systems analysis — will find strong demand. Students who aim only for routine data entry or basic report-building will face increasing automation pressure.

## Slide 15: AI Impact — How IS Engineers Adapt and Lead

### Skills That Stay High-Value

**System Architecture and Design**
AI cannot replace the holistic thinking required to design a banking information system that handles 10 million transactions per day, maintains ACID properties under concurrent writes, replicates across data centres, and meets regulatory audit requirements. This requires IS expertise that AI can assist but not replace.

**Stakeholder Communication and Requirements Engineering**
Translating ambiguous business needs into precise technical specifications requires human intelligence, empathy, and context. AI cannot interview a hospital's nursing team, understand their workflow frustrations, and turn that into a usable information system design. IS graduates who excel at requirements gathering become increasingly valuable as AI handles routine technical execution.

**Data Governance and Quality**
AI systems are only as good as their data. IS engineers who ensure data quality, maintain data lineage, enforce governance policies, and manage master data are essential precisely because AI relies on clean, well-structured data. Garbage in, garbage out — and IS engineers ensure the garbage does not get in.

**Security and Compliance**
AI makes security more complex, not simpler. AI-generated code can introduce new vulnerabilities. AI-powered attacks (deepfakes, AI phishing) require sophisticated defences. IS engineers with security expertise remain critical. India's DPDP Act creates specific human accountability for data security that cannot be delegated to AI.

**AI Integration Skills**
IS engineers who can build the information infrastructure that AI systems run on — databases, APIs, data pipelines, vector stores for RAG systems — are in high demand. This is IS territory: structured data management, API design, and information architecture applied to AI systems.

### The IS Engineer in an AI World

The IS engineer of 2028 is not a manual report-builder. They are the architect of the information infrastructure that organisations and AI systems run on. They design databases that AI queries, build pipelines that AI learns from, secure information systems from AI-powered attacks, and integrate AI capabilities into business applications.

### New Roles Emerging for IS Graduates

- **AI Integration Engineer:** Connects enterprise databases and APIs to LLMs; builds RAG pipelines; designs AI-powered features in business applications
- **DataOps Engineer:** Manages the operational side of data pipelines — monitoring, alerting, data quality checks, CI/CD for data
- **Privacy Engineer:** Implements technical controls for DPDP Act compliance — consent management, data anonymisation, audit logging
- **Prompt Engineer (Enterprise):** Designs and optimises AI workflows for business use cases — not generic prompting, but enterprise-grade AI system design

### Speaker Notes

The correct response to AI disruption in IS is neither panic nor dismissal — it is strategic adaptation. Let me explain what this looks like in practice.

The IS engineer's most distinctive value proposition has always been the ability to design information systems around what an organisation needs to know, not just what it can technically build. This human judgment — about what information matters, how it should be structured, who should access it, and how it should be protected — becomes more important as AI generates technical implementations faster. If AI can generate a SQL schema from a verbal description, the question is: who asks the right questions? Who validates that the schema correctly models the business? Who identifies the edge cases? Who ensures GDPR compliance? That is the IS engineer.

Data governance is a concrete emerging opportunity that is IS-specific and AI-proof. When an organisation trains a machine learning model on customer data, several critical questions arise: Was consent obtained for using this data in AI training? Is the data quality sufficient for the model to be reliable? Can we explain why the model made a specific decision (explainability)? What happens if the model encodes historical bias? These are IS questions. IS engineers who understand data governance frameworks — DAMA-DMBOK, ISO 8000, India's DPDP Act requirements — are the professionals who answer them.

AI integration engineering is an emerging role directly suited to IS graduates. Consider what a RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) system requires: a database of documents or information (IS territory — schema design, indexing), a vector store for semantic search (IS territory — specialised database management), an API layer connecting the LLM to the knowledge base (IS territory — API design and development), a security layer ensuring the AI only accesses information the user is authorised to see (IS territory — access control and security), and a data pipeline keeping the knowledge base updated (IS territory — data engineering). The LLM itself is provided by OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google. The infrastructure it runs on is built by IS engineers.

Students entering IS programs in 2026 should invest in three AI-adaptation skills: first, understanding how AI/ML systems work at a conceptual level and being able to integrate them (not research them); second, learning data engineering tools (Spark, dbt, Kafka) that feed the data pipelines AI systems depend on; third, developing information security depth because AI-powered attacks are making security more important than ever. These investments position IS graduates on the right side of the AI disruption curve.

### Speaker Notes

Real projects are what differentiate IS graduates in the job market. Let me walk you through why the student projects listed here are well-conceived and what makes them strong portfolio items.

The Hospital Information System is arguably the most well-rounded IS final year project you can build. It requires proper ER modelling — patients, doctors, appointments, diagnoses, medications, billing, insurance all have complex relationships. It requires normalisation to avoid redundancy — a patient's name should be stored once, not duplicated across every appointment record. It requires transaction management — when a patient is discharged, the discharge process touches multiple tables (inpatient record, billing, discharge summary) and must either all succeed or all roll back. It requires role-based access control — a doctor sees patient medical records; a billing clerk does not; an administrator sees everything. Building this from requirements to deployed application exercises every IS skill you learn in college.

The Fraud Detection System is strong because it demonstrates ML applied to an IS problem. The dataset is real (Kaggle has public credit card transaction datasets). The business problem is concrete and high-stakes. The technical challenge — class imbalance (fraud is rare, so the model needs to be sensitive to rare events), feature engineering (creating features like transaction velocity, deviation from typical spending pattern), and evaluation metrics (precision and recall tradeoff — a fraud model that flags everything is useless; one that misses fraud is also useless) — mirrors real industry work at banks.

The E-Governance Grievance Portal is interesting because it involves real-world stakeholder engagement. If you actually interview panchayat officers or municipal corporation staff about their current grievance handling process, document it in UML, design an improved system, and build a working prototype, that is genuine IS work. The documentation of your stakeholder interviews and UML models should be part of your project report and showcased in interviews.

The professional projects illustrate where IS graduates work in practice. The Bank Core Banking Migration is one of the most complex and lucrative IS projects in India — moving a bank from one core banking system to another requires mapping thousands of data fields, writing transformation scripts, running parallel systems simultaneously, validating data quality after migration, and managing cutover risk. Senior IS engineers leading these migrations earn significant premiums. SAP S/4HANA implementations employ thousands of IS professionals in India annually, and SAP's own data indicates that 77% of the world's transaction revenue flows through SAP systems — the market for SAP IS professionals is enormous and stable.

The most important advice for student projects: treat them like real work. Do not build toy applications with hardcoded test data and no security considerations. Implement proper authentication, properly normalised databases, error handling, input validation, and role-based access. Write a proper technical design document. Deploy to AWS free tier so you have a live URL to show interviewers. The IS graduate who can say "here is a live system I built, here is the ER diagram, here is how I handled concurrent access" is in a completely different conversation from one who says "I built an HMS but haven't deployed it yet."

## Slide 18: Honest Assessment — Pros and Cons

### Advantages of Choosing IS

✓ **Broad Employability Across Industries**
Every industry that stores and processes data — which is every industry — needs IS professionals. Healthcare, banking, e-commerce, government, manufacturing, education. IS is not dependent on a single sector's health.

✓ **Strong IT Services Demand**
TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Cognizant, and HCL batch-hire IS graduates every year. On-campus placement rates for IS at good engineering colleges are consistently high. Even in IT slowdowns (like 2023's hiring freeze), experienced IS professionals were not as affected as some specialisations.

✓ **Applied, Immediately Employable Skills**
IS is not a theoretical branch. The Full Stack Development, DBMS, Cloud Computing, and Information Security skills you develop are directly applicable from day one. IS graduates adapt quickly to professional environments compared to branches with stronger theory but less applied focus.

✓ **Government IT Pathway (IS-Native)**
NIC Scientist-B, CDAC, and state IT departments are the most IS-friendly government employers. IS skills map directly to the systems these organisations build. The GATE CSE qualification that IS graduates use is the same ticket to NIC that CSE graduates use.

✓ **ERP/SAP Ecosystem — Large and Stable**
SAP consulting is a multi-billion dollar market with high demand and high salaries at mid and senior levels. The market is too large and too domain-specific to be fully automated. IS graduates with 5+ years in SAP are genuinely valuable specialists.

✓ **Certification Ecosystem Directly Maps to Curriculum**
AWS, Azure, Oracle DBA, CompTIA Security+, and Google Data Analytics certifications directly extend what IS students learn in college. The investment in study and exam fees is small relative to the salary premium these certifications deliver.

### Challenges to Know About

✗ **"Lesser CSE" Perception**
At some companies — particularly pure product companies and research organisations — IS is perceived as a lower-tier branch. FAANG-level product companies prefer CSE, DS, or EC graduates for technical roles. This perception is unfair but real and worth knowing about.

✗ **Lower Salary Ceiling than CSE in Top Product Companies**
At the absolute top of the compensation scale — Google L5, Amazon SDE III, Microsoft Principal — CSE/CS graduates from top institutions dominate. IS graduates can reach senior product company roles, but it requires extra proof of technical depth.

✗ **Less Research Scope**
PhD programs and research labs (IIT departments, Google Research, Microsoft Research) have fewer IS-specific opportunities than CSE. If research is your goal, CSE is the stronger foundation.

✗ **Theory-Light Relative to CSE**
Students who want to go deep into theoretical computer science — automata theory, complexity theory, algorithm design at depth — will find IS curriculum shallower than CSE. IS is stronger in applied systems; CSE is stronger in foundations.

✗ **AI Threat to Routine Entry Roles**
As discussed in Slides 14 and 15, IS roles involving manual SQL scripting, basic report building, and simple data entry are at risk from automation. The risk is manageable by targeting higher-value skills, but it requires deliberate choice.

### Speaker Notes

Honest self-assessment of IS as a branch choice requires looking at both the genuine strengths and the real limitations.

The broad employability point is not marketing language — it is genuinely true. I have seen IS graduates work as Systems Analysts at banks, Database Architects at e-commerce companies, IT Managers at healthcare firms, SAP Consultants at manufacturers, Security Engineers at cybersecurity firms, and NIC Scientist-B at government. The range of industries that hire IS professionals has no equivalent in more specialised branches like Metallurgy or even Aerospace. This breadth provides career resilience.

The IT services demand point is important for students from non-IIT/NIT colleges. The reality of campus placements at most VTU colleges is that a significant fraction of IS placements go to TCS, Infosys, and Wipro. These are real jobs with real career paths — not glamorous, but stable and developmental. From a platform perspective, spending 3 years at TCS building database skills and systems analysis experience, then moving to a product company or specialised firm with a stronger profile, is a common and successful career path.

The "lesser CSE" perception is the most painful reality for IS students to hear, but it should inform strategy rather than discourage. At FAANG-style companies, the hiring bar is algorithm-focused and brand-conscious. An IS graduate from VTU competing against a CSE graduate from BITS Pilani for a Google SWE role faces a real disadvantage. The honest response is not to pretend this doesn't exist but to either (a) focus on segments where IS is valued — IT services, BFSI, government IT, ERP consulting, data engineering — or (b) invest in building demonstrable technical depth (strong LeetCode performance, open-source contributions, certifications) that overcomes the brand disadvantage. Both are valid strategies.

The theory-light criticism is fair for students who want deep CS fundamentals. IS does cover Theory of Computation, Algorithms, and OS — but at less depth than CSE programs at IITs and NITs, and with less emphasis on the mathematical foundations. Students aiming for research careers or roles requiring deep systems programming will find IS a weaker foundation than CSE. For industry-facing careers in systems development, data management, and enterprise IT, IS depth is appropriate and sufficient.

## Slide 19: Decision Framework — Is IS Right for You?

### Choose IS If You...

**Like organising, structuring, and managing information** — not just writing code, but thinking about what data an organisation needs, how it should be structured, who should access it, and how it should flow through systems.

**Want to work in industries beyond pure tech** — banking, healthcare, retail, government, manufacturing, insurance. IS skills are transferable across sectors in a way that narrow specialisations are not.

**Prefer applied, project-based work over deep theoretical computer science** — IS is more about building and managing information systems than about advancing computer science theory.

**Are interested in databases, data science, systems analysis, information security, or ERP consulting** — these are the IS specialisations that carry the strongest career trajectories.

**Want a government IT career (NIC, CDAC, state IT depts)** — IS skills map directly to these organisations. GATE CSE qualification gives you the same access as CSE graduates.

**Want to bridge technical and business roles** — Systems Analyst, IT Consultant, Solutions Architect, IT Manager — all require both technical knowledge and stakeholder communication ability. IS trains this combination explicitly.

**Value job stability with reasonable upside** — IS in IT services is a stable, consistently-hiring sector. It will not make you a billionaire, but it will give you a solid professional career with predictable growth.

### Think Twice If You...

**Want to do cutting-edge AI/ML research** — CSE or DS is stronger for this. IS includes ML as a core subject, but the depth is application-focused, not research-focused.

**Are targeting FAANG product roles with maximum compensation packages** — CSE/CS graduates have a demonstrated advantage in Google, Meta, and Amazon SWE hiring. IS graduates can get there, but need to work harder against the perception gap.

**Prefer hardware, embedded systems, or low-level programming** — IS is a software and data branch. ECE or CSE would be stronger for hardware-software interface work.

**Want a branch with strong IIT-level presence** — IS is most prominent at NITs (NIT Surathkal, NIT Calicut, NIT Nagpur) and VTU/private colleges. IITs offer CSE and EE; IS as a named branch is rare at IITs.

### Self-Assessment Questions

1. When you use an app, are you curious about how the database behind it is structured? Does the question "how does the bank know my account balance instantly?" interest you technically?

2. Do you enjoy organising information? Do you naturally create structured lists, organised folders, logical categories?

3. Can you see yourself in a role that requires talking to non-technical stakeholders, documenting their needs, and translating those needs into technical specifications?

4. Are you interested in data — not just as a programmer but as someone who finds meaning in patterns, insights, and decisions derived from information?

5. Do you value breadth of career options across many industries over depth in one narrow technical specialisation?

### Speaker Notes

This decision framework is designed to help you make an honest, informed choice — not to sell you on IS.

The "like organising information" criterion is more diagnostic than it sounds. People who thrive in IS careers typically have a mindset that notices structural problems in information — when a spreadsheet has redundant entries, they want to clean it up; when a process involves looking up the same information in five different places, they want to centralise it; when a system gives inconsistent data to different users, they want to trace and fix the inconsistency. This mindset is different from the "I want to build cool things" instinct that drives many CSE students. Both are valid — but they lead to different careers.

The interest in bridging technical and business roles is particularly important to consider honestly. IS careers, especially Systems Analysis and IT Consulting, require significant client interaction, documentation, and communication. If the idea of spending 30% of your day in meetings — requirements workshops, UAT sessions, stakeholder reviews, project status calls — sounds unappealing, you may prefer a more deeply technical role (DBA, data engineer, security engineer) within IS or a branch like CSE where technical depth is more predominant.

The government IT career path through NIC is genuinely a reason to choose IS. Not because IS is uniquely required — CSE graduates also qualify — but because IS skills are exceptionally well-aligned with what NIC actually builds. Database systems, information security, enterprise applications, systems analysis — these are NIC's core activities, and IS training directly prepares you for them. For students from families that value government service, stable income, and good work-life balance, NIC Scientist-B through GATE CSE is one of the strongest career outcomes available to an IS graduate.

One common scenario where IS is the right choice even for highly capable students: a student who is good at mathematics and programming but also good at communication, enjoys working with people, and is interested in the intersection of technology and business domains (banking, healthcare, government) will thrive in IS. The combination of technical aptitude and communication ability is rarer than pure technical ability alone, and IS careers reward this combination exceptionally well.

### Speaker Notes

Let us close with a clear-eyed summary of what choosing Information Science Engineering means and what you can do with that choice.

The strongest argument for IS is its connector role. Every organisation in the world — from a village panchayat to a multinational bank — has information it needs to manage, secure, access, and derive value from. IS engineers are the professionals who make that happen. They are not the most glamorous technology professionals, but they are among the most continuously needed.
